Our Mission

The Nairobi History Museum is dedicated to preserving and sharing the rich history of Kenya's capital city. From its origins as a railway camp in 1899 to its current status as a major African metropolis, we aim to educate and inspire visitors through engaging exhibits, historical artifacts, and immersive experiences.

Our Story

Founded in 2005, the museum grew out of a research project by Professors Bethwell A. Ogot and Madara Ogot, who authored the comprehensive "History of Nairobi 1899-2012: From a Railway Camp and Supply Depot to a World-Class African Metropolis." Their work forms the foundation of our permanent exhibits.

Collection

Our collection includes over 10,000 artifacts, photographs, documents, and multimedia resources that trace Nairobi's development. Highlights include:

  • Original Uganda Railway construction documents and photographs
  • Historical maps of Nairobi's development from 1899 to present
  • Personal accounts and oral histories from Nairobi residents
  • Artifacts from Nairobi's diverse communities
  • Architectural models of significant buildings

Railway Camp Origins (1899-1920)

The foundation of Nairobi as a railway supply depot

Home > Exhibits > Railway Camp Origins

The Birth of a City

Nairobi originated in 1899 as a railway depot for the Uganda Railway during the construction of the Mombasa-Kampala line. The site was chosen for its central location between Mombasa and Kisumu, its adequate water supply, and its elevation which offered a comfortable climate compared to the coastal heat.

Key Figures

  • George Whitehouse - Chief Engineer of Uganda Railway
  • John Ainsworth - First Sub-Commissioner of Nairobi
  • Lord Delamere - Influential early settler
  • Alibhai Mulla Jevanjee - pioneering Indian merchant

Significant Events

  • 1899 - Railway reaches Nairobi
  • 1900 - Nairobi becomes railway headquarters
  • 1905 - Becomes capital of British East Africa
  • 1919 - Granted municipal status

Early Development

The town was initially divided into European and Asian quarters, with African workers housed in temporary settlements. The first urban plan was created in 1898 by Assistant Engineer Arthur Church, establishing the grid pattern that still characterizes central Nairobi today.

Despite outbreaks of plague and a major fire in 1905 that destroyed much of the original commercial district, Nairobi grew rapidly as an administrative and commercial center, attracting settlers, traders, and workers from across East Africa and beyond.

Colonial Capital (1920-1945)

Nairobi as the administrative center of British East Africa

Home > Exhibits > Colonial Capital

Administrative Center

During this period, Nairobi solidified its position as the capital of Kenya Colony (established in 1920). The city saw significant infrastructure development, including the construction of government buildings, churches, hotels, and the Nairobi Railway Station that became an iconic symbol of the city.

Architectural Development

  • McMillan Memorial Library (1931)
  • City Hall (1935)
  • Norfolk Hotel (1904, expanded in 1920s)
  • Holy Family Cathedral (completed 1952)

Social Structure

  • Racial segregation policies
  • Development of ethnic enclaves
  • Emergence of African political consciousness
  • Growth of trade unions

World War II Impact

During World War II, Nairobi served as an important military base for Allied forces in East Africa. The war brought economic changes and increased urbanization, setting the stage for the independence movement that would follow in the post-war years.

Road to Independence (1945-1963)

Nairobi's central role in Kenya's struggle for independence

Home > Exhibits > Road to Independence

Center of Political Activity

The post-war period saw Nairobi become the epicenter of Kenya's independence movement. The city was home to key political organizations, including the Kenya African Union (KAU) and later the Kenya African National Union (KANU) and Kenya African Democratic Union (KADU).

Key Events

  • 1946 - Jomo Kenyatta returns to Kenya
  • 1952 - Declaration of State of Emergency
  • 1956 - Operation Anvil in Nairobi
  • 1960 - First Lancaster House Conference
  • 1963 - Independence achieved

Important Figures

  • Jomo Kenyatta
  • Tom Mboya
  • Oginga Odinga
  • Dedan Kimathi
  • Harry Thuku

Urban Development

Despite the political turmoil, Nairobi continued to grow as an economic hub. New housing estates were developed for Africans, and the city's infrastructure expanded to accommodate a growing population. The 1950s and early 1960s saw the construction of several iconic buildings that would define the post-independence cityscape.

Modern Metropolis (1963-Present)

Nairobi's transformation into a world-class African city

Home > Exhibits > Modern Metropolis

Post-Independence Growth

After independence in 1963, Nairobi experienced rapid growth as the capital of the newly sovereign Kenya. The city became a major diplomatic hub, hosting numerous international organizations including the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and UN-Habitat.

Key Developments

  • 1963 - Nairobi becomes capital of independent Kenya
  • 1973 - Kenyatta International Conference Centre completed
  • 1970s - Expansion of industrial areas
  • 1990s - Multi-party democracy and economic liberalization
  • 2010 - New constitution and devolved government

Architectural Landmarks

  • Kenyatta International Conference Centre
  • Times Tower
  • UAP Tower
  • Britam Tower
  • GTC Office Complex

Contemporary Nairobi

Today, Nairobi is a thriving metropolitan center known as the "Green City in the Sun." It serves as East Africa's financial hub, with a growing technology sector that has earned it the nickname "Silicon Savannah." The city faces challenges typical of rapidly growing African cities, including infrastructure demands, housing needs, and economic inequality, but continues to evolve as a dynamic center of culture, business, and innovation.
